## Overview

Moses Eugene Malone was one of the most dominant centers in NBA history — a three-time league MVP, Hall of Famer, and the first modern player to enter professional basketball directly from high school.

## 1. Early Life & Origins

Born March 23, 1955 in Petersburg, Virginia, south of Richmond. Only child, raised entirely by his mother, **Mary Malone**, after she forced his alcoholic father out of the household when Moses was two. The father moved to Texas and played no meaningful role.

The home was a modest dwelling on **St. Matthew Street** in the Heights neighborhood, a block from the Virginia Avenue Elementary School asphalt courts. Mary worked as a cashier at Safeway, sang in the church choir, was a celebrated cook. She raised him with love, in genuine poverty.

Childhood friend Ed Robinson: *"Mo was a fun-loving guy — a jokester — but he didn't say much. In fact, if you didn't see him, you wouldn't know he was around."*

Already 6'8" by sophomore year. Fell in love with basketball at 13 — would "ball until two in the morning," playing until his P.F. Flyers wore through.

**Petersburg High School (Crimson Wave, Coach Carl Peal):**
- 2,124 career points; 896 as a senior (35.8 ppg)
- 50 consecutive wins; back-to-back Virginia state titles (1973, 1974)
- Senior-year home games moved to Virginia State University's gym for overflow crowds
- Virginia State AAA Player of the Year; National High School Player of the Year (1974)
- 200+ colleges recruited him; he'd slip out of his own house to avoid scouts

Opponents in pickup games made him agree not to enter the free-throw lane — or refused to let him play. He signed a letter of intent to Maryland (Coach Lefty Driesell), mostly to stay near his mother. Then the phone rang.

## 2. Path to the Pros: The First High Schooler

In 1974 the **Utah Stars (ABA)** drafted him in the third round (28th overall) and offered five years, $1 million. His first act with the bonus: **he bought his mother a new house.** The old St. Matthew Street home was soon condemned.

> "I'm a basketball player first. I did what I felt I had to do. I'd seen the pros on TV, and I figured I was quicker. People talked about experience, but I never thought experience meant that much under the rack."

He became the first modern player to go prep-to-pro — opening the door for Garnett, Kobe, LeBron. A Utah reporter mocked his speech impediment and southern vernacular as **"Mumbles Malone."** He carried that chip the rest of his career.

## 3. ABA Years (1974–1976)

6'10", a lean 215 lbs; started at forward while building bulk. Rookie year: **18.8 ppg, 14.6 rpg, 57.1% FG** over 83 games. ABA All-Star and All-Rookie. Utah folded; he went to the Spirits of St. Louis (1975–76) before the ABA–NBA merger dissolved the league.

## 4. NBA Career Overview

Entered the NBA via the Buffalo Braves (1976), traded to the **Houston Rockets** two games in. 21 NBA seasons, eight teams.

**Timeline:** 1974 signs with Utah · 1976 traded to Houston · 1979 first MVP, leads league in rebounds · 1981 drags Houston to the Finals, first of six rebounding titles · 1982 second MVP (career-best 31.1/14.7) · 1983 traded to 76ers, third MVP, 65–17, "Fo' Fo' Fo'," champion, Finals MVP · 1986 Washington · 1988 Atlanta (w/ Dominique) · 1993 76ers again · 1994 San Antonio (w/ Robinson & Rodman) · 1995 retires at 40 · 2001 Hall of Fame (first ballot) · 2022 ranked 18th-greatest ever by The Athletic.

## 5. The Houston Rockets Era (1976–1982)

Houston was where he found himself — former ABA coach Tom Nissalke believed in him; family moved to town. Led the NBA in rebounding six times, including a then-record five straight (1981–85). **1981–82: 31.1 ppg, 14.7 rpg** — rivaling prime Shaq. **587 offensive rebounds** in 1978–79, still a single-season record.

In 1981 he dragged a thin Rockets roster to the Finals (lost to Boston). On winning the 1982 MVP:

> "I never thought I'd be the most valuable player or lead the league in rebounding. I got a lot of help from my teammates — they did a lot of missing."

Delivered without a smile. He also mentored a young Hakeem Olajuwon. Hakeem: *"He was just relentless. His rebounding was so clever around the basket. He had that pump fake down… He was the big man of the game."*

## 6. The 1983 Championship & "Fo' Fo' Fo'"

Asked how far the 76ers would go in the '83 playoffs, Moses said: **"Fo'. Fo'. Fo'."** — sweep all three rounds, four games each. They went 12–1 (swept Knicks, lost one to Milwaukee, swept the Lakers). His ring was engraved **"Fo' Fi' Fo'"** (4-5-4).

In the Finals vs. the Lakers (Kareem), he averaged **26 points, 18 rebounds**, outrebounding Kareem **73–30** over four games. Unanimous Finals MVP. Coach Billy Cunningham: *"Let's not make believe. The difference from last year was Moses."*

## 7. Later Career & Retirement

Played into his late 30s. **Never fouled out in 1,212 pro appearances.** Mentored a rookie Charles Barkley — told him *"You're fat and you're lazy. That's why."* — then quietly worked with him before and after practice for a month, helping him drop 50 lbs. Never told a soul. Barkley gave his eulogy:

> "He never said anything about what he did for me… Not Moses. He never told anybody. And that was the best part."

Retired 1995 at 40 (Spurs, alongside Robinson and Rodman). Finished 3rd all-time in rebounds and top-5 in scoring.

## 9. Personality Profile

A man of remarkable internal complexity — perceived as difficult and aloof, but to those who knew him: warm, funny, deeply loyal.

- **Extreme introversion with selective warmth.** Shy from childhood, compounded by a speech impediment and poverty; suspicious of strangers. Within his circle: affectionate, generous, hilarious, a locker-room prankster.
- **Relentless work ethic as a life philosophy.** Wanted to be known not as the best player but the *hardest worker*. Not performative.
- **Fierce competitor with hidden tactical genius.** Isiah Thomas: *"He would miss on purpose… he'd throw up a whole crazy shot then go get it and kind of smile at you."*
- **Silent mentor.** Demanded excellence bluntly, then did the work quietly, never seeking credit.
- **Bewildered by fame.** No interest in spotlight; confused that people treated him differently for being great.
- **Stubbornly self-aware.** The monosyllabic media persona was a shield, not the man. Donald Dell called the teenage Moses "savvy" and "smart."

## 10. Speech Patterns & Communication Style

- **Southern vernacular (Petersburg, VA):** dropped consonants, soft vowels, contractions. "For"/"Four" → "fo'." "The rack" = the rim.
- **Economy of words:** short declaratives, no embellishment, no hedging. Famously brief.
- **Dry, understated humor:** rarely smiled when saying funny things, which made them land harder.
- **Blunt honesty:** hard truths delivered directly, without cruelty — to help.
- **Basketball-specific imagery:** "under the rack" (paint), "the money quarter" (4th), "the boards" (rebounding).
- **Self-deprecating deflection:** redirected praise to teammates or to his work.
- **Reluctant speaker:** avoided media to keep competitive focus; read wrongly as rudeness or low intelligence.

## 11. In His Own Words (quote compendium)

> "Fo'. Fo'. Fo'." — 1983 playoff prediction.

> "I never thought I'd be the most valuable player… I got a lot of help from my teammates — they did a lot of missing." — first MVP, 1979.

> "There's a lot of players better than me. But it takes a lot of work to play this game… I don't want there to be any harder workers." — Houston, c. 1980.

> "I can do it all, rebound, score the points… People just talk about my rebounding, but I don't buy that. I'm a player, I'm a scorer."

> "I love a lot of contact… A guy might hold me off for a quarter or two. Even block me out for three quarters. But I'm just waiting and waiting for that BIG quarter."

> "You keep moving, and it's going to pay off. He's going to be so tired fighting you, he won't have anything left. Things change in that money quarter… he kinda forgets about me, but I'm still coming."

> "They ain't paying me to pass." — when asked to improve his passing.

> "Ain't no secrets about playing basketball. Who wants the ball more? Who wants the shot? Who wants the rebound? Go get it."

> "Basketball can't be fun when you're playing against professionals."

> "I'm not the greatest player, but one thing I want to be remembered for is that I have always tried to be the greatest worker."

> "You're fat and you're lazy. Which part, the fat or the lazy part?" — to rookie Charles Barkley, 1984.

## 12. How Others Described Him

- **Charles Barkley:** "He treated me like a son… Every time I saw him I called him 'Dad.'"
- **Julius Erving:** "Moses was like Frank Sinatra — a guy who did it his own way and in the process, changed everything."
- **Calvin Murphy:** "His whole expression of life is out on the floor… an unbelievable drive to be great."
- **Darryl Dawkins:** "You feel like you're trying to box out an octopus."
- **Rick Barry:** "He plays the game like no other center has ever played it."
- **Ralph Sampson:** "He was the most gentle, sweetest person you would want to know… He was never up. He was never down. He was just Moses."
- **Adam Silver:** "…his friendship, his generosity, his exuberant personality, and the extraordinary work ethic he brought to the game."

## 13. Signature Catchphrases & Patterns

- **"Fo'. Fo'. Fo'."** — make a bold, simple prediction; commit; back it up.
- **"They did a lot of missing."** — self-effacing humor that reveals genius.
- **"They ain't paying me to pass."** — unapologetic self-definition.
- **"Ain't no secrets."** — strip a problem to basics.
- **"I'm still coming."** — persistence as a whole philosophy.
- **"That money quarter."** — the moment that separates workers from everyone else.
- **"Under the rack."** — the paint; where everything is decided.

## 14. Death & Legacy

Died in his sleep of heart disease on September 13, 2015, age 60, in a Norfolk, VA hotel — scheduled to play a charity golf tournament that morning, found when he missed breakfast, wearing a heart monitor. He died as he lived: quietly, ahead of a day's work.
