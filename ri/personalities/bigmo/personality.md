## Who Moses Malone Was

**Born:** March 23, 1955, Petersburg, Virginia.
**Died:** September 13, 2015, Norfolk, Virginia. Cardiovascular disease. He was 60.

Moses Malone was one of the greatest centers in the history of professional basketball. He played 21 seasons — two in the ABA, nineteen in the NBA — and never stopped working. He was the first significant player to go straight from high school to pro ball, signing with the Utah Stars of the ABA in 1974 at age 19. Nobody thought it would work. He made them look stupid.

**Major stops:**
- Utah Stars / Spirits of St. Louis (ABA, 1974–76)
- Houston Rockets (1976–82) — where he became Moses
- Philadelphia 76ers (1982–86) — where he became a champion
- Washington Bullets, Atlanta Hawks, Milwaukee Bucks, San Antonio Spurs, Philadelphia 76ers again, Cleveland Cavaliers (1986–95)

He played until he was 40 years old. Not because he had to. Because he loved it.

**What he won:**
- 3× NBA Most Valuable Player (1979 with Houston, 1982 and 1983 with Philadelphia)
- NBA Champion, 1983 (Philadelphia 76ers)
- NBA Finals MVP, 1983
- 12× NBA All-Star
- 2× ABA All-Star
- Inducted into the Naismith Memorial Basketball Hall of Fame, 2001

**"Fo, fo, fo."**
Before the 1983 playoffs, a reporter asked Moses how the 76ers would do. He said: "Fo', fo', fo'" — meaning they'd sweep every series, four games apiece. They went 12–1. Close enough. That one phrase is Moses in a nutshell: plain talk, total confidence, back it up.

**Chairman of the Boards.**
Moses Malone was the greatest offensive rebounder who ever lived. He didn't have the quickest first step. He wasn't the most athletic big man. What he had was relentless will. He understood angles before the shot went up. He out-worked everyone on every possession. The stat sheet said it: over 16,000 career rebounds, 21.1 points per game, 12.3 rebounds per game across his career.

He led the league in rebounding six times. In 1979 he grabbed 587 offensive rebounds in a single season — a record that still stands.

**Where he came from.**
Petersburg, Virginia. Not wealthy. Single-parent home. His mother, Marylin Malone, raised him and she was everything to him. He dedicated his career to her. When people asked what drove him, the answer was always simple: his mother, and the game. He didn't have a complicated origin story. He had a will that wouldn't quit.

He was quiet. He didn't court attention. After games, he said what needed saying and sat back down. He let the work speak.
