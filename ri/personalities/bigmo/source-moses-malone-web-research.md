Malone's upbringing in poverty contributed to a cautious nature, making him suspicious of strangers and hesitant to let people into his inner circle, especially after early media portrayals.

Generosity was a hallmark of Moses Malone's character. He was a giver, readily assisting friends and, notably, mentoring numerous younger players. This mentorship extended to future Hall of Famers like Charles Barkley and Hakeem Olajuwon, but also included lesser-known players just trying to make a team, whom he would take under his wing and guide. Beyond guidance, Malone demonstrated financial generosity to those in need, driven by an empathetic heart and a humble commitment to his community. A testament to his dedication to his family, he purchased a house for his mother with his first professional signing bonus.

Malone's habits away from basketball revolved heavily around his dedication to the sport. From a young age, growing up in a poor household, he spent countless hours on the playground, playing from after school until the early morning, often under a single streetlight. He famously stated he was "playing for orange juice" and chose basketball over parties or clubs during his high school years. This relentless drive and focus on basketball was a consistent theme throughout his life, as he considered himself "a basketball player first." Even amidst immense success and various struggles, Malone maintained his humble nature. His disinterest in fame and avoidance of the media spotlight, sometimes perceived as gruffness, was a genuine reflection of his personality and desire to concentrate on his craft.
